# Dashboard Story
## Executive Sales Dashboard — Retail Business (2024–2025)

---

## The Story This Dashboard Tells

This executive dashboard answers one central question for leadership:

> **"Where is our business making money, where is it losing money, and what should we do about it?"**

The dashboard is structured as a narrative journey through the data — from the big picture down to actionable specifics.

---

## Chapter 1: The Big Picture (KPI Cards)

The dashboard opens with three headline KPIs visible at a glance:

- **Total Sales: ₹21.7 Crore** — a strong revenue base
- **Profit Margin: 15.35%** — healthy but with room to improve
- **Return Rate: 4.55%** — manageable but worth monitoring

These three numbers immediately orient the leadership team. Before diving into any analysis, they know the scale and health of the business.

---

## Chapter 2: Are We Growing? (Sales Trend View)

The sales trend chart (monthly line chart) reveals whether the business is on an upward trajectory.

**Story:** Sales show consistent performance across 2024–2025, with certain months spiking — likely tied to seasonal demand or campaign activity. The trend line confirms positive overall momentum.

**Decision it enables:** Leadership can quickly spot any slowdown months and investigate root causes, or double down on months with historically strong performance.

---

## Chapter 3: Where Are We Strong? (Regional Performance View)

The filled map and regional bar chart show that **South India leads in total sales**, while the **East region delivers the best profit efficiency**.

**Story:** Not all regions are equal. South generates the most revenue, but the East is doing more with less — a sign of better product mix or stronger pricing discipline. The West slightly underperforms relative to its sales volume.

**Decision it enables:** Regional managers can be benchmarked. East's playbook should be studied and replicated. West should investigate margin leakage.

---

## Chapter 4: What's Driving Profit? (Category Profitability View)

The category and sub-category bar chart reveals a stark contrast:

- **Technology = profit engine** (18.2% margin, ₹2.8 Crore profit)
- **Furniture = profit drain** (6.9% margin, loss-making in several sub-categories)
- **Office Supplies = modest contributor** (14.9% margin)

Within Technology: **Copiers, Accessories, and Phones** are the stars.
Within Furniture: **Tables and Bookcases** frequently go into loss territory under heavy discounting.

**Story:** The business is profitable largely because of Technology. Furniture is consuming resources and marketing spend while delivering poor returns.

**Decision it enables:** Leadership can make a strategic call on Furniture — invest to fix it, reprice it, or reduce its footprint.

---

## Chapter 5: Who Are Our Customers? (Segment View)

The segment comparison reveals three distinct customer types:

- **Home Office:** Highest revenue (₹7.45 Crore), but highest return rate (5.7%)
- **Consumer:** Balanced performer with the lowest return rate (3.9%)
- **Corporate:** Solid revenue and return rate comparable to Consumer

**Story:** Home Office is our top-line champion but is also our biggest operational risk. High returns from this segment cost the business money and satisfaction.

**Decision it enables:** Targeted post-purchase support for Home Office customers can reduce returns, improving net profitability without needing to acquire new customers.

---

## Chapter 6: The Discount Problem (Discount vs Profit View)

The scatter plot is the most important chart for business leaders:

- Orders with **no or low discounts** cluster in the **highly profitable zone**
- Orders with **30%+ discounts** show significantly **lower or negative profit**
- The relationship is near-linear: **more discount = less profit**

**Story:** Discounting is eroding the margin. The data shows the business is sometimes paying customers to take products (negative profit orders). This is especially acute in Furniture with high discounts.

**Decision it enables:** Introduce a discount governance policy. No category should be discounted above a profitability threshold without CFO or category manager sign-off.

---

## Chapter 7: Are We Shipping Fast Enough? (Shipping Performance View)

The shipping analysis shows:

- **Same Day and First Class** deliver fastest but are used for a minority of orders
- **Standard Class** (58% of all orders) averages **4.7 days** to delivery
- Longer delivery times correlate with lower customer ratings

**Story:** The majority of our customers experience a 4+ day wait. In a competitive environment, this is a vulnerability — especially as customer ratings are visibly affected by delays.

**Decision it enables:** Evaluate whether upgrading a portion of Standard Class orders to First Class (especially for high-value Technology orders) could improve ratings and reduce returns.

---

## Chapter 8: What's Being Returned? (Return Analysis View)

The return rate heat map by Segment × Category shows:

- **Home Office × Furniture** has the highest return rate
- **Consumer × Technology** has the lowest return rate
- Returns are not evenly distributed — they are concentrated in specific intersections

**Story:** The return problem is not random. It follows a pattern: certain customers buying certain products are more likely to return. This is actionable — it points to product descriptions, quality control, or shipping damage in specific product lines.

**Decision it enables:** Prioritise quality checks and better product descriptions for Furniture items sold to Home Office customers. This is the highest-impact return reduction opportunity.

---

## The Dashboard's Conclusion

The executive dashboard tells a clear and consistent story:

1. **Technology is our profit engine** — protect it, grow it, don't discount it heavily.
2. **Furniture needs strategic review** — particularly Tables and Bookcases with aggressive discounts.
3. **Home Office is our top customer segment by revenue but our biggest return risk** — invest in post-purchase support.
4. **Discounting policy needs tightening** — high discounts directly destroy margin with little compensating volume benefit.
5. **Shipping experience can be improved** — especially for Standard Class orders tied to high-value products.

These five actions, if taken, could meaningfully improve profit margin from 15.35% to 17–18% within 12 months.
